Building and Evaluating AI Products and Agents

Build a real AI product over ten weeks — and prove it works. A project-based course on taking AI products and agents from idea to Demo Day: justifying AI fit, navigating latency–quality–cost trade-offs, and rigorously evaluating your own system.

Overview

An end-to-end build, with evaluation at the center

Weeks 1–3 give every student a solo rep: problem, prototype, evaluation. Teams of four then carry one product through a proposal, a midterm build, and a judged Demo Day. Each weekly session pairs an hour of lecture and reading discussion with an hour of lab: workshop, project work, or guest Q&A.

Format3 units · one 2-hour session per week for 10 weeks
EnrollmentCapped at 48 students — 12 teams of 4
PrerequisitesCS 106A or equivalent; probability/statistics recommended
AudienceOpen to all Stanford students; designed for upper-division undergrads & master's
Demo DayWeek 10, extended session, before outside judges
Time & locationTBA

Diagnose AI fit and make AI-specific trade-offs

Know when AI is the right tool, and when it's a bad fit or overkill. Optimize the trade-offs AI introduces: latency, quality, cost, non-determinism.

Evaluate AI products rigorously

Design eval strategies and name what they don't cover: when LLM-as-judge needs human validation, or when simulated users won't catch real-user failures.

State what you are optimizing for

Turn a product goal into measurable success criteria, the way "help engineers write code faster" becomes suggestion acceptance rate (~30% for GitHub Copilot).

Account for failure

Identify system-level failure modes and design safeguards. Air Canada's chatbot hallucinated a refund policy the airline was then legally bound to honor.

Schedule

Week by week

Deliverables are due at noon the day of class. Expand any week for its focus and readings.

Individual Phase

Weeks 1–3

Every student runs the full loop solo: pick a problem, assess AI fit, ship a prototype, evaluate it.

Wk 01 Why AI products are different

When is AI the right tool, and what is an "AI product"? Reason about the trade-offs of probabilistic systems, treating today's constraints (non-determinism, latency, cost) as examples of a durable pattern, not a fixed list.

Readings
  • Sculley et al., "Hidden Technical Debt in Machine Learning Systems" (NeurIPS, 2015)
  • Yan, "Patterns for Building LLM-based Systems & Products" (2023)
  • Optional: Karpathy, "Software Is Changing (Again)" (YC AI Startup School, 2025)
Wk 02 Product discovery in the age of AI Due: discovery brief
Due: discovery brief with AI-fit assessment

Surface real user needs through interviews and jobs-to-be-done; use AI to synthesize research while judging whether its output is genuine signal or sophisticated noise.

Readings
  • Christensen et al., "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done" (HBR, 2016)
  • Park et al., "Generative Agent Simulations of 1,000 People" (2024)
  • Optional: Hämäläinen et al., "Evaluating LLMs in Generating Synthetic HCI Research Data" (CHI, 2023)
Wk 03 Evals fundamentals Due: working prototype
Due: working prototype of your discovery-brief idea

Build a prototype, then learn to tell whether it's any good: choose evaluation dimensions, score outputs, read benchmarks skeptically (Goodhart, gaming), and weigh human against automated judges. In-class lab: an interactive case study, then a workshop designing evals for your own prototype.

Readings
  • Zheng et al., "Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena" (NeurIPS, 2023)
  • Miller, "Adding Error Bars to Evals" (Anthropic, 2024)
  • Optional: Tan et al., "JudgeBench" (2024) · Tyser et al., "AI-Driven Review Systems" (2024)

Team Phase

Weeks 4–10

Teams of four form in Week 4 and build toward Demo Day, organized around three gates.

Wk 04 Improving the product Gate 1 · proposal
Gate 1 · Teams form + project proposal (~3 pages)

Turn evaluation into an improvement loop: pick the right lever (prompt → retrieval → scaffolding → fine-tune → RL) and apply self-improvement methods, including adversarial and recursive self-improvement, while avoiding optimizing your measure into meaninglessness.

Readings
  • Ouyang et al., "Training Language Models to Follow Instructions with Human Feedback" (InstructGPT, 2022)
  • Yuan et al., "Self-Rewarding Language Models" (2024)
  • Optional: Christiano et al., "Deep RL from Human Preferences" (NeurIPS, 2017)
Wk 05 Multi-turn evaluation and simulation

Judge behavior across whole interactions, and build simulated users to test at scale, anchoring on measuring the sim-to-real gap.

Readings
  • Yao et al., "τ-bench: A Benchmark for Tool-Agent-User Interaction in Real-World Domains" (2024)
  • "Lost in Simulation: LLM-Simulated Users are Unreliable Proxies for Human Users in Agentic Evaluations" (2026)
  • Optional: Anthropic, "Demystifying Evals for AI Agents" (2026)
Wk 06 User research beyond the metrics

Where qualitative research (sentiment, the felt experience, diary studies, AI-generated users) complements granular evals, and where it breaks down.

Readings
  • Amershi et al., "Guidelines for Human-AI Interaction" (CHI, 2019)
  • "The Use of LLMs in HCI: A Critical Analysis of Synthetic Users" (Augmented Humans, 2025)
  • Optional: Weisz et al., "Design Principles for Generative AI Applications" (CHI, 2024)
Wk 07 Case study: when AI products fail Gate 2 · midterm
Gate 2 · Midterm report (~5 pages) + working build

Diagnose real failures as systemic rather than single-cause, and identify the eval, guardrail, or research that would have caught each. Also due: eval feedback for two other teams.

Readings
  • Bainbridge, "Ironies of Automation" (Automatica, 1983)
  • OpenAI, "Expanding on What We Missed with Sycophancy" (2025)
  • Case sourcing: "When AI Fails, What Works? A Data-Driven Taxonomy of Real-World Risk Mitigation Strategies" (2026)
Wk 08 Responsible AI

Red-team for worst-case and harmful behavior, address agentic safety risks such as destructive actions and leaked secrets, and weigh who sets the boundaries on AI use.

Readings
  • Amodei et al., "Concrete Problems in AI Safety" (2016)
  • Ganguli et al., "Red Teaming Language Models to Reduce Harms" (Anthropic, 2022)
  • Lab tool: Debenedetti et al., "AgentDojo" (NeurIPS, 2024)
Wk 09 Case study: voice and multimodal

Evaluate beyond text, where latency, tone, turn-taking, and cross-modal grounding create new failure modes, worked through real product examples.

Readings
  • Clark & Brennan, "Grounding in Communication" (1991)
  • Sierra, "τ-voice: Benchmarking Full-Duplex Voice Agents" (2026)
  • Optional: "MULTIVOX" (EMNLP, 2025) · "Full-Duplex-Bench" (2025) · HEIM (NeurIPS, 2023)
Wk 10 Demo Day Gate 3 · Demo Day
Gate 3 · 12-min demo · final report (5–7 pages) due 72 hrs after class

Defend an evidence-backed ship-or-don't-ship call with your eval results and a concrete next-iteration plan, before outside judges. Extended session. Also due: peer feedback and ratings.

Prerequisites & onboarding

No AI experience required

CS 106A or equivalent is required; probability or statistics coursework (e.g., CS 109, MS&E 120/125, STATS 116, CME 106) is recommended. Optional onboarding modules cover everything else; their hands-on tasks chain into one small app you build, ship, and measure. Complete them, or arrive with equivalent knowledge, by Week 3.

M0 How LLMs actually work ~1–3.5 hrs · self-paced

Watch Karpathy, "Deep Dive into LLMs like ChatGPT" (2025); at minimum the chapters on hallucinations and tool use, “models need tokens to think,” “jagged intelligence,” and RLHF, which ground the evaluation and failure-mode work in Weeks 3–7.

M1 Dev setup: Git and AI coding tools ~1.5 hrs · live

A 20-minute git tutorial, then pick one AI coding tool (Cursor, Claude Code, or Antigravity) and skim its quickstart. Task: build something trivial from a plain-English prompt, then commit and push it to a new repo: a first rep of AI-assisted building before Week 3's prototype lab.

M2 Shipping a prototype: APIs, data, hosting ~1.5 hrs · live

Get API access to a frontier model and make a first call; security basics (API key handling, separating code from customer data); a database quickstart; deploying on a web host. Task: extend your M1 app with a feature that calls a model API and stores the result, deployed to a live URL.

M3 Stats refresher ~30 min · self-paced

Expected value, variance, and confidence intervals, drawn on when scoring outputs and reading error bars in Week 3. Mainly for students without the recommended probability/statistics coursework.

Starter projects

Bring your own idea, or start from one of ours

Teams may pick or adapt a starter project. Each is scoped to a seven-week build and teaches a distinct evaluation lesson.

Meeting notetaker with structured outputs

A desktop assistant that runs alongside calls, captures typed shorthand plus audio, and produces action items and owner-tagged decisions. Hand-label real meetings, then build an LLM judge and measure its agreement with humans before trusting it at scale.

Teaches: LLM-as-judge calibration

First-pass moderation for a real community

Partner with one Discord, subreddit, or Slack and build the first-pass moderator: flag, route to a human, or auto-action. Build separate dashboards for false-positive and false-negative harms and defend your threshold choice.

Teaches: asymmetric error costs

Fantasy draft copilot with model tiering

Live-draft recommendations on fast, cheap inference; weekly start/sit advice with confidence intervals on slower, deeper analysis. Defend the routing decision between model tiers.

Teaches: cost optimization via model tiering
Guest speakers

Learn from the people shipping this

Guest speakers join the lecture hour most weeks; outside judges score Demo Day. Speakers and judges are targeted at a high bar across the AI-product layer:

Founder-CEOs who have shaped AI-era product thinking
Product leaders at frontier AI labs
CEOs of AI-native product companies
Practitioners building AI evaluation infrastructure

The lineup is announced at the start of the quarter.

For Demo Day judges: Week 10 is an extended session. Judges watch 12-minute team demos and score each team's final recommendation, and the evaluation evidence behind it, against the course rubric.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Who can enroll?

Open to all Stanford students; designed primarily for upper-division undergraduates and master's students (MS&E, CS, Symbolic Systems, EE). Enrollment is capped at 48 students — 12 teams of 4.

Do I need AI or machine learning experience?

No. CS 106A or equivalent programming experience is the only requirement. Optional onboarding modules cover how LLMs work, git and AI coding tools, APIs, databases, deployment, and stats; complete them by Week 3.

I'm not a strong coder — will I keep up?

Yes, by design. The onboarding modules get you from CS 106A to shipping an AI-assisted app before Week 3, and the individual-phase prototype is expected to be built with AI coding tools. That's the skill being taught. Teams of four need product judgment, user research, and evaluation rigor as much as code; those are graded criteria, not extras.

What's the time commitment?

3 units: one 2-hour session per week plus project work outside class, intensifying during the team phase (Weeks 4–10) as your team builds toward the midterm and Demo Day.

Do I need my own product idea?

No. Teams may pick or adapt a starter project, or bring their own idea and defend its AI fit in the Week 4 proposal.

How do teams form?

Teams of four form in Week 4, after everyone has shipped and evaluated a solo prototype in Weeks 1–3. The team's project proposal is due the same week.

What's the attendance policy?

Attendance is required, with up to 2 absences, tracked through pop quizzes in every session (including guest lectures). You must earn at least 50% of participation points to pass.

Which AI tools and models will we use?

Students choose their own AI coding tool (Cursor, Claude Code, or Antigravity) and get API access to at least one frontier model during onboarding. Full tooling and infrastructure details are shared with enrolled students.
